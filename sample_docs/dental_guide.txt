COMPREHENSIVE DENTAL CARE GUIDE
A Reference for Patients and Dental Professionals

================================================================================
SECTION 1: CDT CODE REFERENCE
================================================================================

CDT (Current Dental Terminology) codes are the standardized codes used by dental
offices and insurance companies to describe dental procedures. Understanding common
CDT codes helps patients read their treatment plans and Explanation of Benefits (EOB)
statements more accurately.

D0120 — Periodic Oral Evaluation
This code covers a routine check-up for an established patient. The dentist examines
teeth, gums, and oral tissues, reviews X-rays, and documents any changes since the
last visit. Most insurance plans cover D0120 twice per year at no cost to the patient.
This visit typically takes 10–15 minutes and is separate from the cleaning appointment.

D1110 — Prophylaxis (Adult Cleaning)
A professional cleaning performed by a registered dental hygienist. During a D1110
visit, the hygienist removes soft plaque and hard calculus (tartar) from all tooth
surfaces above and below the gumline using hand instruments and an ultrasonic scaler.
The appointment concludes with polishing and fluoride application. Most PPO plans
cover two D1110 cleanings per calendar year at 100%. Patients with active gum disease
may be moved to a more frequent D4910 periodontal maintenance schedule instead.

D2140 — Amalgam Restoration, One Surface
A silver (amalgam) filling placed on one surface of a posterior tooth (molar or
premolar). Amalgam is durable and long-lasting — typically 10–15 years — making it
a cost-effective choice for back teeth. Insurance plans often cover amalgam restorations
at 80% after the annual deductible. If the patient requests a tooth-colored composite
instead (D2391), the plan may only pay the amalgam rate and bill the patient the
cost difference.

D7210 — Extraction, Erupted Tooth Requiring Elevation and/or Forceps
This code covers a surgical extraction of a tooth that requires the dentist to section
the tooth, remove bone, or use a surgical flap to achieve removal. It differs from a
simple extraction (D7140), which involves only forceps with no bone removal. D7210 is
commonly used for impacted wisdom teeth, severely broken-down teeth, or teeth with
curved roots. Post-operative instructions include biting on gauze for 30–45 minutes,
avoiding straws for 24 hours, and taking prescribed medications as directed.

D3330 — Endodontic Therapy, Molar Tooth (Root Canal — 3 or More Canals)
Root canal therapy on a molar tooth with three or more canals. The procedure involves
removing the infected or inflamed pulp, shaping and disinfecting the canals with
nickel-titanium files, and filling the space with gutta-percha. A temporary or
permanent crown (D2740 or D2750) is typically recommended after root canal therapy
to protect the tooth from fracture. Insurance coverage for D3330 varies widely —
many plans cover 50–80% after deductible, but require pre-authorization on procedures
exceeding $300.

SECTION 2: TREATMENT PLAN PRIORITIES
================================================================================

Dental treatment plans are typically organized into three priority categories:

URGENT (Address within 1–2 weeks)
Urgent conditions include acute dental infections (abscesses), severe pain from
pulpitis or a fractured tooth, oral swelling that may indicate spreading infection,
and trauma resulting in avulsed (knocked-out) or luxated teeth. A knocked-out
permanent tooth should be re-implanted within 30 minutes for the best prognosis.
Store the tooth in milk or saline — not water — until you reach the dental office.

MODERATE (Address within 1–3 months)
Moderate priority items include cavities that have reached the dentin layer (moderate
decay), failing or cracked existing restorations, teeth with reversible pulpitis
(sensitivity that resolves quickly), and early periodontal disease requiring scaling
and root planing (D4341). Delaying moderate-priority treatment risks the condition
escalating to an urgent or more complex (and expensive) situation.

ROUTINE (Address within 6 months)
Routine items include small enamel-only cavities, elective cosmetic procedures such
as whitening or veneers, replacing old but functional amalgam restorations with
tooth-colored alternatives, and preventive sealants for children and at-risk adults.

SECTION 3: COMMON PATIENT QUESTIONS
================================================================================

Q: Do root canals hurt?
A: Modern root canal therapy is no more painful than getting a filling. The procedure
is performed under local anesthesia, so the tooth and surrounding area are completely
numb during treatment. Patients may experience mild soreness in the jaw for 2–3 days
afterward, which is easily managed with over-the-counter ibuprofen or acetaminophen.
The pain that drives most patients to need a root canal — the throbbing ache of an
infected tooth — is far worse than the procedure itself.

Q: How long does a filling last?
A: The lifespan of a filling depends on material and placement. Composite (tooth-colored)
fillings last 7–10 years on average; amalgam fillings last 10–15 years. Larger fillings
and those in high-chew-force areas (molars) tend to wear faster. Signs a filling needs
replacement include sensitivity, visible cracks, or food catching around the edges.

Q: Is extraction better than a root canal?
A: When possible, saving the natural tooth with a root canal is almost always preferred
over extraction. Natural teeth are stronger, feel more natural for chewing, and prevent
the adjacent teeth from drifting. After extraction, a dental implant (D6010) or bridge
(D6240) is needed to restore the space, which typically costs more than root canal
therapy plus a crown. However, if a tooth has insufficient bone support, a severe
fracture below the bone level, or repeated root canal failures, extraction may be the
clinically appropriate choice.

SECTION 4: WHAT TO EXPECT AT A CLEANING
================================================================================

A typical adult prophylaxis appointment (D1110) runs 45–60 minutes and follows this
sequence:

1. Medical history review — The hygienist updates your health history and medication
   list. Certain medications (blood thinners, bisphosphonates, immunosuppressants)
   affect dental treatment planning.

2. Periodontal charting — Six measurements are taken around each tooth using a
   millimeter probe. Readings of 1–3 mm are healthy; 4 mm or greater may indicate
   pocketing associated with gum disease.

3. Scaling — Hard calculus and soft plaque are removed from all tooth surfaces.
   Patients with heavier buildup may feel pressure or mild discomfort; numbing gel
   can be applied on request.

4. Polishing — A gritty prophylaxis paste removes surface stains and smooths enamel.

5. Flossing — Interdental spaces are cleaned and flossing technique is demonstrated.

6. Fluoride — A fluoride varnish or gel is applied to strengthen enamel and reduce
   sensitivity. Avoid eating or drinking for 30 minutes after application.

7. Exam — The dentist joins for the D0120 evaluation, reviewing X-rays and performing
   an oral cancer screening (soft tissue exam).

SECTION 5: INSURANCE PRE-AUTHORIZATION
================================================================================

Pre-authorization (also called pre-determination or prior authorization) is a process
where the dental office submits a claim to the insurance company before performing
treatment, so the patient knows their estimated out-of-pocket cost in advance.

When is pre-authorization required?
Most plans require pre-authorization for procedures expected to cost more than $300
per tooth, including crowns, root canals on molars, surgical extractions, and
periodontal surgery. Plans may also require it for implants (D6010), orthodontics
(D8080), and bone grafts (D7953).

How long does pre-authorization take?
Processing typically takes 2–4 weeks. Some carriers offer electronic pre-authorization
with same-day or next-day response. Pre-authorization is not a guarantee of payment —
coverage is subject to eligibility at the time of service and plan maximums not being
exhausted.

What if my plan denies a pre-authorization?
You have the right to appeal. Common grounds for appeal include medical necessity
documentation from the dentist, a letter of dental necessity, and comparisons to
alternative treatments. Ask your dentist's office for help submitting a peer-to-peer
review or formal written appeal.

Annual maximum: Most PPO dental plans have an annual maximum benefit of $1,000–$2,000.
Once that maximum is reached, the patient is responsible for 100% of remaining costs
until the plan year resets (usually January 1). Strategic treatment sequencing across
calendar years can help patients maximize their benefits.

SECTION 6: MANAGING DENTAL ANXIETY
================================================================================

Dental anxiety affects an estimated 36% of the population, with 12% experiencing
extreme dental fear. Left unaddressed, dental anxiety leads to appointment avoidance,
which allows small problems to progress into more complex and costly ones. The good
news is that modern dentistry offers many evidence-based strategies to help anxious
patients receive care comfortably.

Communicate with your dental team
The single most effective step is telling your dentist and hygienist that you feel
anxious. Dental teams are trained to accommodate fearful patients. Establish a stop
signal — such as raising your left hand — so you can pause the procedure at any time.
Knowing you are in control dramatically reduces perceived threat.

Choose a supportive appointment time
Schedule appointments early in the morning when you are less likely to have spent hours
anticipating the visit. Avoid scheduling on days when you have other stressful events.
Some patients find that bringing a trusted support person to sit in the waiting room
provides meaningful reassurance.

Breathing and grounding techniques
Slow diaphragmatic breathing — inhale for four counts, hold for two, exhale for six —
activates the parasympathetic nervous system and reduces the physiological stress
response. Grounding techniques such as the "5-4-3-2-1" sensory method (identify five
things you see, four you can touch, three you hear, two you smell, one you taste) can
interrupt anxious thought patterns during treatment.

Distraction tools
Many dental offices now offer ceiling-mounted TVs, noise-canceling headphones with
music or podcasts, and virtual reality headsets. Bringing your own earbuds with a
familiar playlist is perfectly acceptable and can make a significant difference in
perceived procedure length and discomfort.

Nitrous oxide sedation (laughing gas)
Nitrous oxide (N2O) mixed with oxygen is a safe, mild inhaled sedative delivered
through a small nasal mask. It takes effect within two to three minutes, produces a
pleasant sense of warmth and relaxation, and wears off completely within five minutes
of removal — allowing patients to drive themselves home. Nitrous oxide is available
at most general dental practices and is typically billed as a separate line item
(CDT code D9230). It is safe for children and adults and has an excellent safety record.

Oral sedation and IV sedation
For patients with severe dental phobia or those undergoing lengthy procedures, oral
sedation (a prescription anti-anxiety medication taken one hour before the appointment)
or IV conscious sedation administered by a trained anesthesiologist provides deeper
relaxation. Patients must arrange a driver when using oral or IV sedation. Not all
dental practices offer these services; ask for a referral to a sedation dentistry
specialist if needed.

Building a long-term relationship
Anxiety tends to decrease significantly once a patient builds trust with a consistent
dental provider. Scheduling short, low-stakes "get acquainted" visits — such as
a cleaning with no other treatment — before committing to a complex procedure allows
the patient to experience the office environment positively. Many patients who begin
with severe anxiety become routine visitors once they feel genuinely heard and respected
by their dental team.
